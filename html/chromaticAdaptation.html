Overview

This MATLAB tutorial was created to model the phenomenon of chromatic adaptation in the human visual system. When you stare at a certain color for long enough, your eyes 'adapt' to this color and detection of this color is subsequently diminished. This can cause a striking visual illusion if you look at a new, similarly colored image, as it takes an increased amount of the color to produce the same color perception.

Opponent Color Theory

According to color opponent theory, some cells (on a higher level than cone cells) in the human eye respond more strongly to red, blue, or yellow. This mechanism could help your eyes adjust and discriminate colors in different light spectra and levels. To review, there are three cone types in the retina of your eye that respond to color: S (short), M (medium) and L (long). Each of these has a frequency range in which the the cones respond which together make up the visible spectrum.

Setting Up the Model



Here we assign random cone activation levels in an arbitrary range [-1500,1500] to gives us a theoretical starting point for L,M & S cones.






Information about cone activation is used to determine whether the response to blue or is stronger for a small sample of cells (we'll focus only on blue-yellow opponency for now). For blue-yellow, we evaluate cone activations with L + M - S. We'll categorize >= 0 as yellow and < 0 as blue.

Simulate Cone Adaptation







To simulate adapting to the color blue, we reduce sensitivity of the S cone (responds to the 'blue' part of the visible spectrum) by 25%. Then we calculate the resulting blue-yellow activation and see that blue is reduced, resulting in more cells responding to yellow.



Making the Image





Having both opposing colors side by side makes the adaptation easy to detect, with each eye adapting to a different color.




It's interesting to test the illusion with different filters, but we'll use the high saturation image for the demo. Stare at the black dot in the middle of the adaptor for about 30 seconds, then click on the image to see the image. Be sure to keep staring in the middle of the image after you click to keep your eyes in the same orientation as when they adapted!
